Abstract

Base registries are trusted authentic sources of information under the control of an appointed public administration or organisation appointed by government. Maintaining a base registry comes with extra maintenance costs to create the dataset and keep it up to date. We study the possibility to entangle the maintenance of base registries at the core of existing administrative processes, to reduce the cost of maintaining a new data source. In this paper, we demo a way to manage local council decisions as linked data which creates a new base registry for mandates. We found that no extra effort was needed in the process by local administrations. We show that an end-to-end approach for Local Council Decisions as Linked Data is feasible. Furthermore, using this proof of concept, we established a momentum to roll out these ideas for the region of Flanders in Belgium.

Introduction

Councils are empowered by law, to make decisions on many matters of importance to their local communities. Decisions are made in formally constituted council meetings. Local government data, digitizing these decisions, is locked in data silo's and therefore manually registered in the authorative data source of the regional government. Imagine a smart city where public decision-making is easy for all to follow using any digital channel. s.a. "the effects of local tax policy" or an insight in "the public procurement".

This pilot is a stepstone in the transition of the Flemish Agency for Domestic Governance towards an administration with a greater focus on policy development in terms of better administrative processes.

Related work

Within European policies today, there is on-going work towards base registries, trusted datasets, authoritative sources and appointing responsible parties to collect certain information.

The European Commission defines a base registry (BR) as a trusted authentic source of information under the control of an appointed public administration or organisation appointed by government [1]. Trusted means that, in this case, the local government is managing the local council decisions conformant to best practices in all EIF-domains [2], more specific at the level of semantic and organizational interoperability and conformant to legal requirements in the Municipal Decree [3]. On the semantic level we’ve defined a vocabulary for council decisions and at the organizational level we’ve organized working groups with experts from local and regional governments, where they have agreed on decentralized processes which provide data to the base registry, in line with the principles of linked data. Authentic means that this is considered to be 'the' source of information which represents the correct status and which kept constantly up-to-date and is of highest possible quality. This is archieved by avoiding to copy the information manually into a separated form or application. When the registered data is part of the core processes of the local administrations and used in their own information systems, we expect this will improve the quality. Appointed means: that the governing administration, the Flemish Agency for Domestic Governance, has a legal basis to collect and maintain the respective information.

Method and implementation

We interviewed X service providers for their stance on Linked Data, interviewed Y local governments on how they register and publish local council decisions. We also organized 3 workshops which formed the input for a software engineer to create a proof of concept.

The domain concepts were formalized into two Linked Data vocabularies: one for the metadata and one for describing public mandates. The proof of consists of 5 components: an editor for local decisions, an HTML-page publishing service responsible for URI dereferencing, a crawler for local decisions and 2 reuse examples on top of the harvested data.

We have introduced a virtual local government called Vlavirgem for which we can publish local decisions. The editor at lblod.github.io/editor is a proof of concept of such an editor, which reuses existing base registries. You can choose to fill out a certain template for decisions that occur often, such as the resignation of a local counselor or the installation of a new one. When filling out the necessary fields, the editor will help you to fill out the necessary fields: indeed, only the people that are currently in office can resign. When finished, you will still be able to edit the official document, which contains more information such as links to legal background, context and motivation, and a category.

Conclusion

Although local council decisions contain authentic and qualitative information in the form of non-structured information, the information in the authoritative source for local mandates (one of the use cases of local council decisions) which is still filled in manually by local governments is lagging and not qualitative.
Other studies Lies VAN CAUTER - SBOV evidence? of Flemish Road Sign Database Project - GEEN TOEGANG - evidence? [4] confirm that when information is collected too far from the source, or the data collection is seperated from the core processes, this has a negative impact on the quality of the authoritative sources of information.
The interviews with local governments indicated that information from local decisions (eg appointing a representative) could be transformed into a structured way, this will reduce the workload caused by reporting to external partners (regional government, re-use by the private sector) and also will increase the internal reuse of this information (eg. to publish local representatives on the website or register in the back-office systems for managing their loans.
The proof of concept shows that (I) an end-to-end approach, based on the developed linked data method, is reachable and the process of making decisions will be more efficient: less manual work, governments may seek easier in regulation, the linked data allows easy to do an impact analysis when legislation is amended. (II) We notice a quality gain in editing due to correct legal references (even referencing to decisions of their own municipality) and the use of qualitative factual data (eg. Addresses linked to the Central Reference Address Database, a regional base registry) (III) Finally, there are also efficiency gains in the publication of the decisions that are automatically published on the website of the local government, in the the codex and without additional efforts suitable for reuse by third parties (Open Data).
The pilot and these insights created a political basis to build a base registry for local council decisions in line with the best practices of this study.
